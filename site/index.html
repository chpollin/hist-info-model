Making the formalized theory of historical information modeling experienceable through interactive visualizations

P1

Temporal Irreversibility

The described states of affairs cannot be re-observed.

P2

Trace Mediation

All access to the referent runs exclusively through traces.

P3

Interpretive Constitution

Historical information does not exist independently of interpretation.

These three primitives are analytically distinct and each necessary. From them, eleven derived properties and twenty-four formal requirements follow. This website lets you explore the derivation structure, evaluate how existing ontology approaches address these requirements, and experience what is lost when requirements are not met — particularly the modeling of absence.

Positive Bias

Ontologies describe what exists, not what is missing. There is no established pattern for "this should be here but isn't."

Open World Assumption

In OWL, missing data means "unknown" — not "informationally significant." Historical research needs both.

Missing Formalization

Philosophy discusses absence (Heidegger, Sartre), but this hasn't been translated into formal ontology patterns.

Context

About This Project

Research Prototype

This website validates 24 formal requirements for historical information modeling against seven existing ontology approaches. It makes visible the five systemic gaps — three of which concern the modeling of absence.

Promptotype

This website is itself a Promptotype — built using the Promptotyping method, an iterative context-engineering approach for data-driven research artifacts with frontier LLMs. The method amplifies domain expertise asymmetrically.

Theory

The formalized theory of historical information is based on three irreducible primitives (temporal irreversibility, trace mediation, interpretive constitution) from which eleven properties and twenty-four requirements are derived.
